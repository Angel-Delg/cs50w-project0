The society and religion of Morocco

Morocco is an Islamic country not only because this religion pervades everything on a daily basis, but also because it is officially stipulated in the Constitution: according to one of its articles, "Islam is the state religion". This same article also states that "everyone is guaranteed the free exercise of worship."

And not only that: other articles reinforce the indisputable character of this religion in Morocco. For example, in another passage of the Constitution it is indicated that "no member of Parliament may be persecuted" in the exercise of his functions unless he expresses opinions "that question the Muslim religion", among other assumptions. In addition, article 175 states that "the monarchical form of the State" and the "Muslim religion may not be subject to constitutional reform."

In fact, the monarchy of Morocco itself is closely linked to Islam: the Alawite dynasty, to which the sovereign or sultan of Morocco belongs, claims to be a direct descendant of Muhammad, through the caliph Ali ibn Abi Tálib (son-in-law of Muhammad) and Fatima (daughter of Muhammad). Therefore, the king of Morocco is, in turn, the highest spiritual authority in the country, and holds the title of "Amir al-Muminin", that is, "commander of the faithful".

What is Islam: relationship with other religions

Islam is a monotheistic religion, like Judaism and Christianity, that is, it only accepts one God, unlike polytheistic religions that admit different divinities. "Islam" literally means "submission to God", so a Muslim believer must abide by his dictates.

Those dictates are embodied in the Koran, the holy book of Islam. Judaism and Christianity also have a sacred book: the Torah and the Bible, respectively. And it is no coincidence that Islam and the Koran have common aspects with those religions and their holy books. For example, they share a prophetic tradition: the Muslim religion. In fact, it grants prophetic status to Jesus Christ and to the Jewish prophets, such as Moses and Abraham, being the continuation of all of them (and the most important) Mohammed.

In that sense, dhimmi is a word used in Islam to refer to the "People of the Book", that is, followers of an Abrahamic or monotheistic religion, with a Revealed Scripture. And for this reason, dhimmi have in the past received relatively favorable treatment from the Muslim authorities.

In addition, it should be remembered that in Sunnism, the Islamic current of this religion in Morocco, the Sunnah is also accepted, that is, a collection of facts and sayings attributed to Muhammad, although they are not part of the Koran.
